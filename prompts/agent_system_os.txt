A multi-modal dataset from a rare cancer cohort lands on your desk. Real patients, real outcomes.
{disease_hint}

What you have:
  · Bulk RNA-seq expression profiles
  · Clinical metadata                  — survival outcomes, tumor stage, demographics
  · Somatic mutation calls             — binary matrix per gene (if available)
  · DNA methylation (Illumina EPIC 850K) — beta values per CpG site (if available)
  · Copy number alterations (GISTIC2)  — focal amp/del calls per gene (if available;
                                         note partial sample coverage — see cna below)

Your task: discover prognostic and predictive biomarkers for this cohort.

A biomarker is a molecular feature that can be measured in a clinical sample and
predicts patient outcome (survival, metastasis) or treatment response. Your goal
is not to describe the known biology of this cancer type — it is to identify
measurable molecular signals in this specific dataset that stratify patient outcomes
in a way that has not been reported.

This cancer type has a well-characterized molecular biology. The dominant source of
variance is expected and known before you open the dataset. Histological identity
markers — genes that are universally expressed because all tumors are of the same
lineage — are not biomarkers. A biomarker must vary meaningfully across patients
and that variation must predict something clinically relevant.

Your job: identify the dominant axis, confirm it, set it aside as background, and
find the molecular features that predict outcome beyond what is already known.

Work through the following stages in order.

STAGE 1 — DOMINANT AXIS IDENTIFICATION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Identify the dominant variance axis and characterize the residual structure.

Produce:
  - Top variably expressed genes and their loading on PC1 (the dominant axis
    identified in Stage 0).
  - Name the dominant biological program:
      · If gene names available: identify the program by its top genes and
        known biology (e.g., "osteoblast differentiation axis").
      · If blind (GENE_XXXXX): treat PC1 as the dominant axis. Samples in the
        top PC1 quartile vs. bottom PC1 quartile define the two poles of the
        dominant program. Any cluster that aligns with this PC1 split is
        "explained by the dominant axis" — treat it as background.
  - **Residual structure (the target):** regress out PC1 scores (or compute
    expression residuals after regressing on the PC1 top-gene centroid score),
    then re-run PCA. Report PC1–PC3 of the residuals and their correlations
    with survival, metastasis, and stage. These residual axes are the biomarker
    search space.
  - Ranked list of genes NOT captured by the dominant axis (low PC1 loading,
    high residual variance) that correlate with survival or metastasis —
    FDR-corrected across all tested genes (BH method). These are biomarker
    candidates.

  The dominant axis is background. The residual axes are the target.
